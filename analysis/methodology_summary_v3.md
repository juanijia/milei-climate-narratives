# Methodology Summary v3: Milei's Climate Narratives

**Date:** June 2026  
**Version:** 3.0  
**Previous version:** `analysis/methodology_summary_v2.md` (v2)

---

## Overview

Phase 3 v3 applies a refined version of the v2 analytical framework, maintaining its two-dimensional theoretical architecture while clarifying the **data-source separation** between the two dimensions. The core change from v2: Dimension 1 (Discursive Delegitimization) is applied exclusively to the **speech corpus**; Dimension 2 (State Capability Erosion) is applied exclusively to **formal documentation** (executive decrees, budget records, administrative reorganization documents, official policy instruments). This separation was implicit in the v2 design but not enforced during coding, which led to D2 codes being applied to speeches when Milei referenced or announced institutional changes. V3 corrects this.

---

## The V3 Analytical Framework

### Two corpora, two dimensions

The article analyses **two distinct data corpora** to demonstrate the dual mechanism:

**Corpus A — Presidential Speeches (this codebook)**  
Speeches, keynote addresses, interviews, and public discourses where Milei articulates and performs his ideological project. The unit of analysis is the rhetorical passage. Coded with **Dimension 1** only.

**Corpus B — Formal Documentation (separate analysis)**  
Executive decrees, budget documents, administrative reorganization records, official policy instruments, and official communications with international bodies. The unit of analysis is the formal institutional act. Coded with **Dimension 2** only.

The dual mechanism is demonstrated by triangulating the two corpora: Corpus A shows *how* the rhetoric delegitimizes sustainability governance; Corpus B shows *what* is institutionally dismantled. The article then traces the co-articulation between them.

---

### Dimension 1 — Discursive Delegitimization (Corpus A: Speeches)

Captures how Milei's discourse problematizes, delegitimizes, or reframes the role of the state, sustainability governance, and scientific expertise. Three sub-dimensions:

**D1.1 — Delegitimization of State Intervention and Sustainability Governance**  
Market primacy arguments; anti-state/anti-bureaucratic rhetoric; "la casta" framing applied to sustainability actors; delegitimization of environmental regulation; deregulation-as-benefit narratives; framing sustainability as ideological or harmful; rejection of multilateral governance.

**D1.2 — Delegitimization of Public Science and Expertise**  
Attacks on public research institutions (CONICET); criticism of universities; delegitimization of expert authority as a class; climate skepticism and dismissal of environmental science; framing science as ideologically captured or pseudo-scientific; rejection of expert mediation in policymaking.

**D1.3 — Woke / Cultural Backlash**  
Ideological bundling of climate with "wokeism," feminism, and other progressive causes; neo-Marxism framing of environmentalism as disguised socialism; civilizational defense framing positioning climate governance as one front in a civilizational war.

**The key mechanism in D1:** *Contested legitimacy.* Milei does not need to prove that climate change is false or that regulations are technically ineffective. He needs to delegitimize the institutional actors, epistemic authorities, and governance frameworks that would make state climate action publicly justified. D1 codes map these delegitimation moves.

---

### Dimension 2 — State Capability Erosion (Corpus B: Formal Documentation)

Captures concrete institutional and budgetary changes that erode the state's practical capacity to govern sustainability transitions. Three sub-dimensions:

**D2.1 — Budgetary Retrenchment**  
Budget cuts to scientific institutions (CONICET, research programs); cuts to environmental agencies; reductions in climate- and sustainability-related programs; cuts to public universities.

**D2.2 — Institutional Downgrading and Administrative Restructuring**  
Reorganization or downgrading of ministries and secretariats (e.g., ministry → secretariat); agency elimination or merger; changes in regulatory institutions; staffing reductions and administrative hollowing-out.

**D2.3 — Sustainability-Policy Dismantling**  
Changes in formal climate commitments (NDCs, international agreements); withdrawal from international initiatives; changes in policy instruments; deregulation affecting environmental protection; reduced implementation or enforcement of existing policies.

**The key mechanism in D2:** *Implementation gap.* Even if formal climate commitments remain nominally in place, eroding the administrative, regulatory, scientific, and fiscal capacities required to implement them constitutes effective policy dismantling. D2 codes document this erosion through formal institutional evidence.

## Inductive Categories (Speech Coding)

Three inductive categories from prior coding passes are carried forward for v3 speech coding:

- **Selective_Empiricism** — appropriating empiricism for pro-market claims while denying it to climate science. Closest D1 code: 1.2 > Pseudo_Science_or_Ideological_Science.
- **Temporal_Appropriation** — co-opting intergenerational moral language against sustainability governance. Closest D1 code: 1.1 > Rejection_of_Multilateral_Governance.
- **Reported_Denial** — inter-discursive corroboration from opposing speaker. Not primary discourse; confirmatory only.

## Key Analytical Questions for Phase 3 v3

1. **Distribution across D1 sub-dimensions:** How does Milei's rhetoric distribute across D1.1 (state/governance), D1.2 (science/expertise), and D1.3 (cultural)? Is the primary mechanism epistemic (attacking the knowledge base for action) or political (attacking the institutional actors)?

2. **Rhetorical evolution over time:** Does the balance across D1.1/D1.2/D1.3 shift across the corpus (2023–2026)? Does early rhetoric rely more on D1.3 (cultural war framing) while later speeches develop more sophisticated D1.1 arguments (foundational economics)?

3. **Institutional announcements re-coded as D1:** In v3, passages where Milei announces or justifies institutional changes (e.g., exiting the 2030 Agenda, calling for reduction of supranational organizations) are coded as D1 — capturing the rhetorical work of those announcements. Do these passages cluster in particular D1 sub-codes (e.g., Rejection_of_Multilateral_Governance) across speeches?

4. **Inductive category formalization:** Do the inductive categories (Selective_Empiricism, Temporal_Appropriation) recur consistently enough across the corpus to warrant promotion to permanent deductive codes in a future revision?

5. **Triangulation with D2:** Once the formal documentation corpus is coded with D2, how does the rhetoric in Corpus A map onto the formal actions in Corpus B? Do high-intensity D1.1 speeches precede or accompany D2.3 formal actions?

## Why v3? The Methodological Clarification

### What changed in v2 coding (the problem)

In v2, several speech passages were coded under D2 categories — specifically D2.3 > Withdrawal_from_International_Initiatives and D2.2 > Agency_Elimination_or_Merger — when Milei referenced or announced institutional actions in his speeches. For example:

- *"La Argentina decidió en su momento apartarse de este proceso"* (ONU 80th, 2025) was coded D2.3.
- *"The drastic reduction of all supranational organizations"* (Davos 2025) was coded D2.3.
- The UN four-principle proposal (ONU 80th, 2025) was coded D2.2.

### Why this was incorrect

These passages are **discursive acts**, not institutional acts. When Milei announces "Argentina has decided to exit the 2030 Agenda," the speech is performing the rhetorical work of delegitimizing multilateral governance and framing the exit as justified — this is Dimension 1. The institutional act itself (the executive decree, the official communication to the UN, the voting record) is the Dimension 2 evidence, and it comes from formal documentation, not from the speech.

Conflating the two creates several analytical problems:

1. **It blurs the mechanism.** The article's core argument is that libertarian governance operates through a *dual* mechanism — discourse delegitimizes, formal action dismantles. If the same data source provides evidence for both, the dual-mechanism claim cannot be demonstrated empirically; it is simply asserted.

2. **It misrepresents the nature of speech data.** Presidential speeches are rhetorical performances. Even when they reference institutional changes, they are accounts, justifications, or announcements — not the changes themselves.

3. **It understates the rhetorical richness of D1.** Passages that were coded D2 in v2 also carry strong D1 signals: announcing an exit from a multilateral framework while framing it as a principled stand against socialist governance is simultaneously Rejection_of_Multilateral_Governance and Sustainability_as_Ideological_or_Harmful. The D2 code crowded out the more analytically precise D1 coding.

### What the correction produces

In v3, those formerly-D2 passages are re-coded under the appropriate D1 sub-codes. This:
- Makes the speech analysis more analytically precise (capturing the rhetorical work of institutional announcements)
- Reserves D2 for formal documentation where it properly belongs
- Strengthens the article's dual-mechanism argument by keeping the two data sources methodologically distinct
