Good afternoon, everyone.

I stand here before you to state categorically that Machiavelli is dead.

For years, our thinking was distorted by the presentation of a false dilemma in the design of public policies, one in which we were supposedly faced with a choice between political efficiency on the one hand and respect for the ethical and moral values of the West on the other.

As Professor Jesús Huerta de Soto points out in his work on dynamic efficiency, from that perspective, efficiency is not compatible with various schemes of equity or justice, but rather arises solely and exclusively from one of them, the one based on respect for private property and the entrepreneurial function.

Therefore, the opposition between the dimensions of efficiency and justice is false and erroneous. That is to say, what is just can't be inefficient, nor can what is efficient be unjust. Actually, from the standpoint of dynamic analysis, justice and efficiency are two sides of the same coin.

`[CODE: D1.1 > Market_Primacy]` — *Memo: The Huerta de Soto dynamic efficiency argument performs a crucial rhetorical and theoretical function: it collapses the efficiency-justice distinction that underpins much environmental economics. In standard welfare economics, a carbon tax may be efficient (correcting an externality) but creates distributional concerns (justice). Milei's framework eliminates this tradeoff: what is just (private property, voluntary exchange) is by definition efficient, and what is efficient cannot be unjust. This forecloses the standard argument for environmental regulation — that a market failure produces an inefficient and unjust outcome that state intervention can improve. In the v2 framework, this is Market_Primacy deployed at the level of foundational economic philosophy.*

However, this required embracing a mathematical structure that left the door open to state intervention under the well-intentioned goal of correcting market failures, which, from my perspective, do not really exist.
To address this, the proof developed by Hans-Hermann Hoppe, based on property rights in line with Locke's principle of original appropriation together with the non-aggression principle, not only proves satisfactory in establishing optimality, but also leaves no room for intervention.

For climate governance, this is a foundational argument: the theoretical framework that justifies carbon pricing, emissions trading, and environmental regulation (neoclassical externality theory) is replaced by a rival framework in which intervention is not merely inadvisable but logically prohibited. The political theory dimension (Hoppe, Locke) gives this a natural-law authority that the earlier economic arguments lacked.*

In turn, in 2025, I explained the mental parasites sown by the left in humanity.
Therefore, today more than ever, in the face of the ethical and moral degradation afflicting the West as a result of embracing the new socialist agenda, it is necessary to once again promote the ideas of freedom.

The values-based view of capitalism holds that if such a position has been achieved through discovery, voluntary exchange and without violating the non-aggression principle, there is no justification for intervention. Indeed, intervention constitutes a violation of property rights, and by punishing profits, the economy's potential growth declines, so intervention and regulation are dynamically inefficient because they are violent and therefore unjust.
